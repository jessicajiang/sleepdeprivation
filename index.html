I'm Intrigued!

What is sleep?

What is sleep?

Let's be honest, we all love sleep and know what it is very dearly. Sleep is technically "a condition of body and mind such as that which typically recurs for several hours every night, in which the nervous system is relatively inactive, the eyes closed, the postural muscles relaxed, and consciousness practically suspended." But really, we know sleep as what we need every night in order to maintain our good health. It's what our body needs to "reset" itself everyday.

How much sleep should I get?

Depending on your age:

  1. Toddlers, Ages 0-6: Hours of sleep recommended to be around 10-14 hours (except for newborns, who need 14-17 hours each day, which is a lot if you think about it!)
  2. Children and teenagers, Ages 6-17: 8-11 hours a day
  3. Adults, ages 18-65+: 7-9 hours
Of course, the actual amount varies from person to person, but we can all agree that the amount seems quite a lot compared to what we all get!

Physiological Benefits

So what are the benefits of sleep? Why is it that my body responds to sleep the way it does?

Physiological Benefits

Sleep is involved in the repair and healing of your heart and blood vessels. This means the more sleep you get, the less chance of heart disease, kidney disease, high blood pressure, and diabetes!

Psychological Health:

Your emotional well-being is linked to sleep as well. Sleep improves your levels of stress, your overall mood, and your ability to make good decisions.

Mental Health

Sleep rests your mind, and a well-rested brain means improved learning! This means that you can take on the day's activities with a smile :)

Why does our body sleep?

Sleep is part of the body's homeostasis. From the time we wake up, our body is slowly accumulating our drive for sleep, which usually peaks in the late evening at around the time we fall asleep. The sleep-inducing hormone, adenosine, builds throughout the day and acculumates to the point where it becomes difficult to resist.

What happens when we sleep that makes our body so...happy?

Sleep is the time when the body secretes many important hormones. By many, it's literally hormones that affect every aspect of the body, like stress, growth, reproduction, appetite, and weight. It's no wonder our body is happier with sleep, because not only is it a time for us to be lazy and do nothing, but our body gets to reset and prepare for a new day!

STAGES OF SLEEP

Stage 1

The first stage of sleep, where one transitions from being awake to falling asleep. Brain waves slow down and people experience a "falling" sensation, hence "falling asleep."

Stage 2:

Period of light sleep where eye movements stop, heart rate slows, and body temperature decreases.

Stage 3/Stage 4

"Slow wave sleep" - characterized by slow brain waves called delta waves. Blood pressure falls, breathing slows, and body temperature drops lower. This is when the body is immobile. (the stage where it's impossible to wake anyone up)

SLEEP DEPRIVATION

What does it mean to be sleep deprived?

The dictionary definition of sleep deprivation "is the condition of not having enough sleep; it can be either chronic or acute. A chronic sleep-restricted state can cause fatigue, daytime sleepiness, clumsiness and weight loss or weight gain." Basically, when one doesn't sleep as much as they should, they are messing up many parts of their body.

What happens when I am sleep deprived?

There are many negative effects of sleep deprivation:

  • Mood: Lack of motivation, anxiety
  • Performance: Lack of concentration and energy, forgetfulness
  • Health: high blood pressure, diabetes

MORE HEALTH BENEFITS OF SLEEP

These are more specific, smaller health benefits that sleep provides us.

Improved Memory

Refine skills when you sleep

Through a process called consolidation, you strengthen memories you learn while you are awake. This means that instead of trying to stay up cramming for a test or exam, sleeping might be the better option!

Live Longer

Sleep improves quality of life

Because sleep releases so many hormones essential to our survival, it's only natural that those that sleep more will be overall healthier! In a 2010 study of women ages 50 to 79, more deaths occurred in women who got less than five hours or more than six and a half hours of sleep per night.

Be Creative!

Spur your creativity!

Sleep solidifies emotional components of memories and your attention, which overall contributes to more creative thinking. After all, creative thinking is hard enough when completely awake. No one likes their ideas when they've pulled an allnighter!

Be A Winner!

Win!

Sleep obviously takes away from fatigue during the daytime, which adds to more stamina. Harvard and Stanford studies have shown that football and tennis players have improved times over those that did not sleep at least ten hours a night.

Improve your grades

Get that 4.0!

Sleep increases focus, concentration, attentiveness, and learning. In a study for college students, those that didn't get enough sleep had a lower average GPA than those who did. According to Dr. Rapoport, "If you’re trying to meet a deadline, you’re willing to sacrifice sleep...but it’s severe and reoccurring sleep deprivation that clearly impairs learning."

Lose Weight

Diet by sleep?

Instead of eating those #kalesalads, plan an earlier bedtime instead! Sleep and metabolism are controlled by the same parts of the brain, so when one is tired, hormones that affect not only your fatigue but your appetite are spiked.

CAFFEINE

What does Caffeine do?

Caffeine messes with that one hormone mentioned earlier, adenosine, that induces sleep. It blocks the adenosine receptor almost in the same way drugs block dopamine receptors in the brain. Also similar to how drugs work, this is why caffeine becomes highly addictive. However, though there are mainy studies that say people perform better while drinking coffee, caffeine seems to improve speed, not power. That means while you could rush through some brainless things better while drinking coffee, the actual amount and quality of work will be lowered.

The effectiveness of coffee varies from person to person as well and wears down over time. As one continuely takes in caffeine, the body and mind build up a tolerance to it. Thus, the more someone drinks caffeine, the more they will need it in the future as well.

Caffeine's half life - or the amount of time it takes for half of an ingested dose to wear off - is about five to six hours on a human. Interestingly, women taking birth control take twice as long to process the caffeine, while those who are ovulating have an extended half-life as well. Smokers have caffeine processed twice as fast.

A caffeine withdrawl usually begins anywhere from 12-24 hours after your last use. Sometimes these withdrawls are accompanied by headaches, fatigue, lethargy, nausea, and vomiting, as well as eye muscle spasms. You'll only really feel completely better in around ten days, which is actually a very long time.
